# AREI · Broker Go-to-Market — Cape Verde
## Cape Verde Real Estate Index · Market 01

**Status:** Internal strategy memo · pre-outreach
**Date:** May 2026
**Audience:** Founding team
**Scope:** First 3–5 broker conversations in Cape Verde

This is not a sales script pack. It is a strategy memo for the first real-world broker conversations. The goal is to validate the model, understand broker concerns, and test whether the exchange is credible. Follow-up templates and scripts come after this phase.

---

## 1. Strategic objective

We are not trying to close deals. We are trying to learn.

The first 3–5 broker conversations have one job: find out whether the AREI broker relationship is viable before we build more infrastructure around it.

**What we want to learn:**

- Do brokers understand what AREI is once it is explained simply?
- What is their first concern — listing accuracy, competition, brand, lead quality, or something else?
- Do they see value in international visibility, or do they already have it?
- Are they open to correcting or improving how their listings appear?
- Would any of them share a direct feed or cooperate as a registered source?
- What does a broker in this market actually need that AREI could provide?

The conversations are research, not sales. If a broker asks to participate, that is a bonus. If they raise a concern we hadn't thought of, that is the win.

---

## 2. Broker psychology

Before the first conversation, assume nothing. Most brokers in a fragmented market like Cape Verde have been approached by portals, lead-gen platforms, and aggregators before. They are not naive about what these relationships usually look like.

**What they may worry about:**

**Losing control of listings.**
If AREI is already publishing their listings, they did not consent to that. The first instinct may be: who authorised this, and can I stop it?

**Wrong prices or stale listings.**
A listing that appears with an outdated price or inaccurate details reflects on the broker, not the platform that published it. This is a real and legitimate concern. Do not dismiss it.

**AREI becoming a competitor.**
If AREI sends buyers directly to listings, why would a buyer use a broker? If AREI becomes a portal, it starts eating into their business model. This fear is reasonable even if the reality is different.

**Lead capture.**
If a buyer finds a property through AREI and AREI captures the inquiry, does the broker get the lead? Or does AREI monetize it separately? The mechanics of lead flow matter to them.

**Brand misuse.**
Their agency name, logo, or contact details appearing on an external site without permission — especially if the site looks low-quality or inaccurate — is a reputation risk.

**Inaccurate representation.**
A listing that shows the wrong price, wrong location, wrong number of bedrooms, or wrong contact information is worse than no listing at all. They know this from experience with other platforms that have scraped badly.

None of these concerns are unfair. The broker cooperation model only works if we take them seriously, not explain them away.

---

## 3. The exchange

The relationship has to be worth something to both sides. If it is only worth something to AREI, it is extraction, not cooperation.

**What brokers get:**

- **Attribution.** Their listings appear with their agency name, logo if provided, and contact details. The listing points back to them — not to AREI as an intermediary.
- **International visibility.** Cape Verde Real Estate Index is built for the audience that brokers struggle to reach: diaspora buyers, European investors, international researchers who find the market online before contacting anyone locally. AREI distributes directly to that audience.
- **Buyer intent.** The index attracts people actively researching Cape Verde property — not passive social media scrollers. Visitor intent is higher than most listing platforms in this market.
- **A lightweight lead inbox (in development).** Inquiries from index traffic can be routed directly to the broker. No intermediary, no lead-auction model.
- **A local market benchmark.** Brokers will be able to see how their listings perform relative to the local market — average prices by island, listing quality scores, time on market data.
- **Accuracy control.** Registered source brokers get a correction path. If a listing is wrong, they can fix it. Non-registered brokers rely on the parser getting it right.

**What AREI gets:**

- **Better data quality.** A direct feed or registered source relationship produces cleaner records than a parser running against a broker's website. Fewer errors, fewer stale listings, better completeness scores.
- **Source cooperation.** A broker who knows AREI exists and agrees to cooperate is a stable source. A broker who doesn't know AREI exists is a fragile one — their site structure can change, their listings can disappear, and there is no way to flag problems.
- **Market coverage.** 17 sources are mapped in Cape Verde. Not all of them are well-structured. Relationships with the most active brokers improve the coverage and reliability of the index.
- **A defensible publishing model.** An index published with source cooperation is harder to challenge than one published purely from scraping. As the index grows in authority, having registered sources matters institutionally.

**Why both sides benefit:**

The broker's core problem is not finding local buyers — it is reaching the international buyer who is researching online at 11pm in London or Lisbon before they have ever contacted anyone in Cape Verde. AREI is the surface for exactly that buyer. The broker's listing, attributed to them, is the answer to that buyer's question.

AREI's core problem is data quality at scale. Source cooperation solves it faster and more cheaply than parser maintenance alone.

The exchange is real. It does not need to be oversold.

## 4. First conversation framing

The goal in the first two minutes is to avoid sounding like a portal, a lead-gen company, or a tech startup pitching a product they do not understand yet.

**Plain English version:**

"We're building a property market index for Cape Verde — similar to how you'd describe a stock market index, but for property. We track asking prices, listing volumes, and market movement by island, so international buyers can understand what the market looks like before they start making calls.

We index listings from public sources — broker websites, portals, public feeds. Your listings are already in the dataset. We're here because we want to make sure they appear accurately, with your name on them, and that any inquiries from people who find your listings through us go directly to you.

We're not a portal. We don't sell properties. We don't take a cut of anything. We're a data and indexing company. The index is free to read. The relationship we're offering you is simple: we give you visibility with the international buyer audience, you get attribution and a direct contact path for any inquiry. If something is wrong with how a listing appears, you tell us and we fix it."

That is the core. Do not add more than this in the first meeting unless they ask. Let them respond before expanding.

---

## 5. What not to say

These phrases either create legal exposure, sound dishonest, or set expectations AREI cannot meet.

**"We scraped your listings."**
Accurate but immediately antagonistic. Use "we index public listing data" or "your listings appear in our public dataset." The substance is the same. The framing is not.

**"We already listed your properties."**
Same problem. "Your listings are already in our index" is better, but only say it after establishing what AREI is and why it matters to them.

**"We can guarantee you leads."**
AREI cannot guarantee leads. The lead inbox is in development. Index traffic creates buyer intent, not guaranteed inquiries. Do not promise a volume or a timeline.

**"We cover the full Cape Verde market."**
17 sources mapped. That is significant but not complete. Do not imply complete market coverage where it does not exist. If they ask, be specific about what is in the index and what is not.

**"Think of us like a marketplace."**
A marketplace implies a transactional role AREI does not play. It also sounds like a competitor. AREI is an index, not a marketplace.

**"We'll build you a CRM."**
Broker Tools v1 is scoped to listing management, publication, and a lead inbox. It is not a CRM. Do not imply it is, or that it will be.

**"We're building the MLS for Africa."**
Accurate in spirit but loaded with implications about exclusivity, cooperation mandates, and industry politics that AREI is not positioned for. Avoid this framing entirely until the model is much more established.

## 6. Validation questions

These are the most important questions to ask in the first 3–5 conversations. Not all in one meeting — spread them across conversations. Listen more than you speak.

1. **What percentage of your buyers come from outside Cape Verde?** (Tests whether international visibility actually matters to them.)

2. **How do international buyers typically find you — referrals, portals, search, social?** (Reveals the current discovery path and where AREI fits or doesn't.)

3. **Which portals or aggregators are you currently using, and are you happy with them?** (Surfaces existing frustrations with the model AREI is replacing or supplementing.)

4. **When a listing is wrong somewhere online, what usually happens?** (Tests their sensitivity to data accuracy and their current experience with bad scraping.)

5. **Do you have a structured way of sharing listings beyond your own site?** (Opens the source cooperation conversation without asking for it directly.)

6. **What would make you confident a listing attributed to you is accurate?** (Defines the accuracy standard they will hold AREI to.)

7. **If an international buyer found one of your listings through an index like this and filled in a contact form — how would you want that to reach you?** (Tests the lead inbox mechanics and their preferences before we build them.)

8. **Is there anything about how your listings appear online that you'd want control over?** (Opens the correction and branding conversation.)

9. **What would concern you about appearing in an index like this?** (The most important question. Ask it directly. Their answer is the product research.)

10. **Would you ever share a direct feed of your listings with us, rather than relying on us indexing your website?** (The source cooperation ask. Only ask this after trust is established — probably not in the first meeting.)

## 7. Pilot logic

A pilot is not a contract. It is a low-commitment test that gives both sides something real to evaluate.

**What a sensible pilot looks like:**

**Source attribution from day one.** The broker's agency name, contact details, and if they provide it, their logo, appear on every listing attributed to them. They can see exactly how their listings appear before agreeing to anything.

**A direct contact path, not a captured lead.** Any inquiry from an index listing goes directly to the broker's preferred contact method — email or phone. AREI does not intermediate the inquiry or capture the contact details for its own use.

**No lock-in.** The broker can ask for their listings to be removed at any time. AREI has a documented removal process. Public listings will be re-indexed if they remain publicly accessible, but the broker relationship is always voluntary.

**A correction and improvement process.** If a listing is wrong — wrong price, wrong location, stale — the broker can flag it and AREI corrects it within a defined window. Registered source brokers get priority correction access.

**Optional source cooperation.** If the broker wants to share a direct feed, that is the next step, not the first. The pilot does not require it. It should make the value of source cooperation obvious enough that they ask about it.

**No payment in the pilot.** The free tier is the pilot. Paid professional features are not part of the initial conversation.

## 8. Success criteria

A good broker conversation is not one that ends in a handshake. It is one that produces useful signal. Here is what good looks like:

**They understand the model without much explanation.** If the plain English framing lands in under two minutes, the product narrative is working.

**They are not defensive.** Some resistance is normal. But if they are curious rather than closed, the relationship is viable.

**They care about international buyer visibility.** If they immediately say "we already reach international buyers," test whether that is actually true. If it is, they are less interesting as early partners. If it is aspirational, AREI has something to offer.

**They want better lead quality, not more leads.** A broker who wants 100 more inquiries is less useful than a broker who wants 5 serious buyers. AREI is not a volume lead-gen platform. Brokers who understand quality over quantity are the right early partners.

**They are open to source cooperation.** Even if they don't commit to it, if they ask "how would that work?" the relationship has somewhere to go.

**They ask to correct or improve their listings rather than remove everything.** This is the clearest positive signal. A broker who wants to improve their representation understands the model and sees value in it.

## 9. Risks

**Risk: A broker demands their listings be removed.**
This is their right. AREI has a removal process. Do not argue. Remove the listings from the index, document the request, and note what drove it. If the concern is data accuracy, that is a product signal. If the concern is competition, that is a positioning signal.

**Risk: A broker claims AREI is misrepresenting their properties.**
Take it seriously immediately. Ask them to specify what is wrong. Correct it. This is exactly why the correction process exists. A broker who has a bad experience before AREI's methodology is mature will not come back.

**Risk: A broker shares the conversation publicly — social media, broker group chats.**
Assume every broker conversation is semi-public in a small market like Cape Verde. Do not say anything in a broker meeting that you would not want shared in the local industry. The positioning should be consistent: data company, not competitor, international visibility, exchange not extraction.

**Risk: A broker asks for a revenue share or payment.**
AREI does not pay brokers for listing data in the current model. Free distribution is the value exchange. If a broker asks for payment, acknowledge it and explain the model clearly. Do not promise anything. If there is a pattern of brokers expecting payment, that is a market signal worth logging.

**Risk: A broker asks AREI to stop indexing all public listings, not just theirs.**
This is a legal and strategic question, not a sales question. Do not negotiate it in the field. Log it, escalate it, and respond in writing. AREI's indexing of public data is covered by standard web indexing practice, but specific markets and specific data types may have local norms worth understanding.

**Risk: A broker asks to see the methodology before cooperating.**
Good. Methodology v1.0 publication is a near-term milestone specifically because it enables this conversation. If methodology v1.0 is not yet published, be honest: "We're finalising it. Here's what it covers." Do not show a draft methodology as if it is final.

## 10. Decisions needed

These are open questions that need founder decisions before broker outreach scales beyond the first 3–5 conversations.

**1. Removal policy.**
What is AREI's current policy if a broker demands their listings be removed? Is there a documented process? Who owns the decision? This needs to be clear before the first conversation, not after the first complaint.

**2. Correction SLA.**
If a broker flags a wrong listing, how quickly does AREI correct it? One business day? 48 hours? This needs to be a real number, not "as soon as we can." It sets the expectation for what a registered source relationship means.

**3. Lead inbox readiness.**
The lead inbox is in development. At what point is it ready to offer as part of the broker conversation? Offering it before it is built creates an expectation that could damage trust if it slips. Define the release condition.

**4. Source cooperation terms.**
If a broker agrees to share a direct feed, what are the terms? Attribution, usage, removal rights, data freshness — these need a simple one-page document before any source cooperation is formalised. Not a contract — a term sheet.

**5. Which brokers to approach first.**
The 17 mapped sources in Cape Verde are not equal. Some are high-volume, some are niche, some are primarily local, some are primarily international. Who are the first three targets? The answer should be based on listing volume, international buyer focus, and whether the broker has an existing digital presence that signals openness to online distribution.

**6. Who makes the first contact.**
Is the first broker conversation a founder conversation, or is it handled by a future partnerships hire? For the first 3–5 conversations, this should be the founder. These conversations are research, not outreach. The founder needs to hear the concerns directly.

**7. Methodology v1.0 timing.**
Several broker objections — "how do I know this is accurate?", "who validates the data?" — are easier to answer with methodology v1.0 published. If methodology publication is close, it may be worth waiting. If it is months away, the conversations can still happen but with the explicit caveat that the methodology is in progress.

**8. What counts as a successful pilot.**
Define success before the pilot starts, not after. How many listing corrections indicates a healthy relationship? How many months of stable source cooperation counts as a validated pilot? How many broker conversations does it take to validate or invalidate the exchange model?
